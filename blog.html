Proof of the Week #2

Representation theory, July 23, 2023
Let $p \in \mathbb{N}$ be a prime number and $\mathbb{F}_p$ the field with $p$ elements. Consider the group $\mathrm{G}=\mathrm{GL}_2\left(\mathbb{F}_p\right)$ and its subgroups $$ \mathrm{B}:=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{ll} a & b \\ 0 & d \end{array}\right) \mid a d \neq 0\right\} \subset \mathrm{G}, $$ and $$ \mathrm{T}:=\left\{\left(\begin{array}{ll} a & 0 \\ 0 & d \end{array}\right) \mid a d \neq 0\right\} \subset \mathrm{B} . $$ Let $\widetilde{\left(\chi_1, \chi_2\right)}$ be the one-dimensional representation of $\mathrm{B}$ corresponding to the above character of $\mathrm{T}$ under the bijection $\varphi$ from the set of one-dimensional representations of $B$ to the set of irreducible characters of $T$ via the restriction of $\widehat{F_p^{\times}}$. Then the representation $\operatorname{Ind}_{\mathrm{B}}^{\mathrm{G}}\left(\widetilde{\left(\chi_1, \chi_2\right)}\right)$ is irreducible if and only if $\left(\chi_1, \chi_2\right) \neq$ $\left(\chi_2, \chi_1\right)$.

Proof:

We begin with the following assumption; say that $Ind_B^G\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)}$ is irreducible; then recall that by Mackey's irreducibility criterion, it is equivalent to the following two one-dimensional (thus irreducible) representations to be disjoint; namely, \begin{equation*} Res_{B\cap sBs^{-1}}^{B}\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)} \ \text{and} \ Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{B\cap sBs^{-1}}\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)^s}. \end{equation*} More precisely, \begin{equation} \langle Res_{B\cap sBs^{-1}}^{B}\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)}, Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{B\cap sBs^{-1}}\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)^s} \rangle = 0. \end{equation} Observe that $\mathcal{R} = \{1,s\}$ is a complete set of representatives of the double coset $B \backslash G/B$, where \begin{equation*} s = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*} Thus, given any $b \in B$, \begin{equation*} sbs^{-1} = s \begin{bmatrix} x & y \\ 0 & z \end{bmatrix} s^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} z & 0 \\ y & x \end{bmatrix}; \end{equation*} which is in $B$ $\iff$ $y = 0$; i.e. \begin{equation*} b = \begin{bmatrix} x & 0 \\ 0 & z \end{bmatrix} \in T. \end{equation*} Hence, $B \cap sBs^{-1} = T$. Moreover, let $\varphi$ be the bijection given. Then we may write $Res_{B\cap sBs^{-1}}^{B}\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)}(b) = Res_T^B \varphi((\chi_1,\chi_2))(b)$, and \begin{align*} Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{B\cap sBs^{-1}}\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)^s}(b) &= Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{T}\varphi((\chi_1,\chi_2))(s^{-1}bs); \\ &= Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{T} \varphi(\chi_2)(x) \varphi(\chi_1)(y) \\ &= Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{T}\varphi((\chi_2,\chi_1))(b). \end{align*} Here, it follows that in order for the two restrictions to be disjoint, they must not be equal; otherwise, since they would both be irreducible (and equal) their inner product would be 1. Thus; \begin{equation*} Res_T^B \varphi((\chi_1,\chi_2)) \ne Res^{sBs^{-1}}_{T}\varphi((\chi_2,\chi_1)) \iff (\chi_1,\chi_2) \ne (\chi_2,\chi_1). \end{equation*} Hence we obtain that $Ind_B^G\widetilde{(\chi_1,\chi_2)}$ is not irreducible $\iff$ $(\chi_1,\chi_2) \ne (\chi_2,\chi_1)$.

Proof of the Week #1

Homological algebra, July 16, 2023
Let $K$ be a field. Let $\mathcal{I}$ be the category with $\mathrm{ob} \mathcal{I}=\mathbb{N}$ and $$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{I}}(i, j)= \begin{cases}\{\star\} & j \leq i \\ \emptyset & j>i\end{cases} $$ Consider the diagram $$ R: \mathcal{I} \rightarrow \mathbf { CRing }, i \mapsto R_i:=K[X] /\left(X^i\right), $$ (where for $j \leq i$ the map $K[X] /\left(X^i\right) \rightarrow K[X] /\left(X^j\right)$ is the quotient map). Then $\lim _{\mathcal{I}} R_i$ exists in $\mathbf{CRing}$, and it is isomorphic to the power series ring $K[[X]]$.

Proof:

We want to show that there exists isomorphisms \begin{equation*}\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{CRing}}(-,K[[X]]) \underset{\eta}{\overset{\sim}{\longrightarrow}} lim_{\mathcal{I}}\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{CRing}}(-, K[X]/(X^i)).\end{equation*} Where, we recall that $lim_{\mathcal{I}}\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{CRing}}(T, K[X]/(X^i))$ is precisely the set \begin{equation*} \{ (t_i: T \rightarrow K[X]/(X^i))_i | \forall \varphi: i \to j, X(\varphi)\circ t_i = t_j\}. \end{equation*}Consider the map \begin{align*} \left( f : T \rightarrow K[[X]]\right) \mapsto (f_1,f_2,\dots); \end{align*} where $f$ is defined by $a \mapsto \sum_{i=0}^{\infty}b_iX^i$; and $f_j$ is defined by $a \mapsto \sum_{i = 0}^{j-1}X^{\overline{i}}$. Next, let $\star : i \to j$ ($j \leq i)$ then $X(\star): K[X]/(X^i) \to K[X]/(X^j)$ is given by $\sum_{k=0}^{i-1}b_kX^k \mapsto \sum_{k=0}^{j-1}\tilde{b}_kX^k$. Then, observe that \begin{equation*} X(\star) \circ t_i(a) = X(\star)\left(\sum_{k=0}^{i-1}b_kX^k\right) = \sum_{k=0}^{j-1}\tilde{b}_kX^k = t_j(a); \end{equation*} thus the map $\eta$ is well-defined. Next, it is easy to see that it is a homomorphism; and moreover, as given any $f:T \to K[[X]]$, $(f_1,f_2,\dots)$ always exists as $1,X,X^2,\dots$ forms a basis for $K[[X]]$ and $(K[X]/(X^0),K[X]/(X^1),\dots)$. Therefore, $\eta$ is injective. Finally, for $(f_1,f_2,\dots)$ in $lim_{\mathcal{I}}\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{CRing}}(T, K[X]/(X^i))$, we can create an infinite sequence $(a_1,a_2,\dots)$, and a morphism $\psi:T \to K[[X]]$ such that $\eta(\psi) = (f_1,f_2,\dots)$. Hence, $\eta$ is a bijective homomorphism; in other words $lim_{\mathcal{I}}R_i$ exists and is isomorphic to $K[[X]]$.
